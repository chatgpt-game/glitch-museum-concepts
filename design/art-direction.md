# Art Direction

This document outlines the aesthetic vision for **The Glitch Museum**, shaping the look and feel of every environment, character, and UI element. The goal is to blend nostalgic retro visuals with surreal glitch art to create a space that feels both familiar and unsettling.

## Core Visual Themes

- **Retro Low‑Poly Aesthetic:** Draw inspiration from late 1990s console hardware like the PlayStation. Environments and characters should use intentionally low polygon counts and simplified textures reminiscent of early 3D games. - **Glitch Art:** Integrate deliberate visual glitches such as texture corruption, RGB channel shifts, VHS static, and vertex distortion to convey the world’s instability. These effects should be dynamic, triggered by player actions or narrative events.
- **Digital Museum Architecture:** The museum’s layout mixes classical architecture (columns, marble floors) with digital motifs (floating panels, translucent data streams). Exhibits are holographic or screen‑based, blending physical and virtual elements.
- **Lost Media & Internet Archaeology:** Include posters, icons, and user interface elements inspired by GeoCities sites, old chatrooms, and vaporwave aesthetics. The Internet Archaeology wing can feature pixel art banners, 256‑colour GIFs, and low‑resolution textures.
- **Environmental Storytelling:** Use environmental details such as developer notes scribbled on walls, debugging overlays left active, and half‑rendered props to hint at the incomplete nature of the game.

## Colour Palette

- **Muted Base:** Greys, beiges, and dusty blues evoke abandoned hardware labs and file archives.
- **Accent Colours:** Neon pinks, cyan, and glitch greens represent corruption and digital interference. These are used sparingly to draw attention to interactive objects or dangerous areas.
- **Adaptive Lighting:** Lighting should shift based on room stability—warm white light in stable areas and harsh flickering reds/greens in corrupted zones.

## Character Design

- **NPCs:** Use stylised, simple geometry with exaggerated features. For example, the **Glitched Curator** might wear a digital museum uniform that intermittently pixelates. **Corrupted Guides** can have visible artefacts like missing polygons or flickering textures.
- **Player Avatar:** The player may not see themselves directly, but their shadow or reflection could appear as a glitch silhouette, reinforcing their intangible presence.

## UI & HUD

- **Diegetic Interfaces:** Menus and HUD elements should appear as part of the in‑game universe (e.g., debug consoles, sticky notes on monitors). The **Glitch Lens** interface uses wireframe overlays and CRT scanline effects.
- **Readable Typography:** Choose monospace or pixel fonts for code snippets and old‑style sans‑serif fonts for narrative text. Ensure accessibility for players with dyslexia by providing font alternatives.

## Inspirations & References

- **Early 3D Games:** Titles like *Silent Hill*, *Resident Evil*, and *Kings Field* for their atmospheric environments and technical limitations.
- **Vaporwave & Net Art:** Artists who remix retro computer graphics and web aesthetics into surreal compositions.
- **Digital Preservation Museums:** Real institutions like The Museum of Art and Digital Entertainment (MADE) inform how exhibits can be presented interactively.

## Technical Considerations

- **Scalable Fidelity:** Although the art references PS1 limitations, modern players may appreciate smoother controls and higher resolution. Use post‑processing effects (e.g., dithering, colour banding) selectively so they can be toggled on/off.
- **Performance:** Low‑poly models and simple shaders will help maintain high frame rates even when glitch effects overlay the scene.
- **Accessibility Options:** Provide settings to reduce screen shake, adjust colourblind modes, and disable intense visual glitches without compromising the overall aesthetic.

The art direction should evoke nostalgia while embracing the beauty of imperfection. By fusing retro visuals with contemporary glitch art, **The Glitch Museum** will immerse players in a world that feels like an interactive piece of digital history gone awry.
